The Correctable Record

A Compact Handbook on Corrigibility, Reliance, and Diagnostic Governance

The institution did not merely make a diagnosis; it allowed a provisional diagnostic frame to acquire durable reliance while the record itself preserved reasons why that reliance should have remained corrigible.

Description

A medical record can be detailed and still fail to remain correctable.

The Correctable Record is a compact handbook about a diagnostic-governance case centred on corrigibility and reliance. It is not a pleading, not legal advice, not medical advice, and not a memoir. It is a guide to how a provisional diagnostic frame can become clinically, administratively, statutorily, and interpretively durable while the record itself preserves reasons why that frame should have remained open to correction.

The book does not argue that later genetic confirmation should simply be projected backwards into 2017. Its concern is narrower and stronger: what the documents already contained, what the institution allowed those documents to do, and whether later contradiction was used to correct the record or absorbed by the frame.

At its centre is one proposition:

The institution did not merely make a diagnosis; it allowed a provisional diagnostic frame to acquire durable reliance while the record itself preserved reasons why that reliance should have remained corrigible.

This is a field guide to that proposition.

Formal Disclaimer

This book is a compact explanatory handbook.

It is not legal advice. It is not medical advice. It is not an expert report. It is not a pleading. It does not determine liability, breach, causation, limitation, diagnosis, professional misconduct, regulatory outcome, damages, or remedy.

The book is intended to help readers understand a documentary sequence and the concepts of corrigibility, reliance, diagnostic finality, and record governance. Any legal, medical, regulatory, or evidential conclusion must be reached only by properly qualified professionals after review of the full record and applicable law.

Nothing in this book should be read as alleging malice unless a source document independently supports that allegation. Nothing in this book should be read as saying every clinical act was negligent. Nothing in this book should be read as demanding erasure of historical records. The central concern is correction, qualification, supplementation, restriction, contextualisation, and safe future reliance.

Source Discipline Note

This book proceeds from documents.

The documents are not treated as decorative support for a story already decided. They are the terrain. The book asks how the documents should be read, what sequence they disclose, what they record, what they omit, what they allow to become durable, and what they leave unresolved.

The book also recognises a central asymmetry. The institution made and controlled the clinical record at the crucial time. The patient later had to rely on that record to understand what had happened. For that reason, the record must be read carefully, but not passively.

A document may be accurate as archive and misleading as governance.

That distinction is one of the reasons this book exists.

Source-Note Policy

This handbook uses light source anchoring.

The notes are not intended to turn the book into a pleading, exhibit bundle, or expert report. They exist so that counsel and legally literate readers can see where the central documentary moments come from.

The note system follows four rules:

  1. Anchor only key factual moments.
  2. Avoid footnoting every argumentative sentence.
  3. Use source notes to identify documents, not to reproduce long extracts.
  4. Preserve the distinction between source material and analysis.

Where a note is attached to a factual proposition, it means: this is the documentary location from which the moment is drawn. It does not mean the legal conclusion has been proved.

1. Patient's Note on the Record

This is my medical experience. It is part of my history. It is not my story.

My story is far bigger than this section of my life, and far more accommodating than it. Until the middle of March 2025, I trusted my doctor, my neurologist, my general practitioner, and the candidate advanced nurse practitioner. I trusted them with my life. I trusted them so completely that I was not keeping score.

That trust matters.

It means this book does not begin from suspicion. It does not begin from grievance. It does not begin from a wish to find fault in every clinical act, every letter, every omission, or every decision made in uncertainty. It begins from the fact that I lived inside the record before I ever tried to challenge it. I relied on it because I relied on the people and institutions that made it.

For that reason, this book proceeds by documents.

There is no allegation or point made here that is not grounded in the record, or expressly identified as patient recollection where the record is silent. In the final analysis, this case will come down to how the documents are read. The documents should be allowed to speak for themselves. At the same time, it should be remembered that the patient relies on those documents too. They are not merely institutional records. They are also the patient's route back through what happened.

That route back did not exist for me while I was living through the events themselves.

I did not know what the record would later show. I did not know what had been written, what had not been written, what had been acted on, what had not been acted on, or what had been allowed to become durable. I did not know that certain moments would later matter legally, clinically, administratively, or evidentially. I did not know that the words used inside the record would later govern my access to care. I did not know that what was absent from the record might one day matter as much as what was present.

I was a patient.

I was not keeping a parallel file.

That is why the later discovery of the documents changed the situation. It did not simply give me more information. It changed the meaning of inaction.

The case has not been forced on me in the sense that I have no will, no choice, or no agency. I do not say that. I remain free not to take it. I remain free to stop, to leave the matter alone, to accept the institutional position, or to live with the record as it stands.

But freedom does not make every choice equal.

Once the record had shown what it had done, and once later events had shown that the record was still capable of governing my access to care, silence became a different kind of act. Not taking the case would not restore neutrality. It would allow the existing structure to remain in place. It would allow the record to continue carrying authority without the correction, qualification, restriction, or contextualisation that later events appear to require.

That is the position I now find myself in.

I did not go looking for a case. I went looking for care. I went to my GP because my feet, walking, body, and working life were failing. I entered the hospital pathway through an open problem. I left with a lifelong diagnostic frame. For years, I lived inside that frame. I trusted the clinicians. I trusted the record. I accepted, as far as I could, the explanations given to me.

The position changed when the foot pain reversed.

The effect of Sinemet CR in March 2025 did not, by itself, prove every legal or clinical question. It did not retrospectively decide the case. It did not mean that every earlier decision was wrong. But it did something I could not ignore. It returned me to the founding territory of the case: the feet, the walking, the pain, the original problem that had brought me into care.

It also forced me back into the documents.

When I later discovered, through access to records, that during the 2023 crisis my GP had tried to intervene in terms far more serious than I had understood at the time, the meaning of the sequence changed again. I had been the person in crisis. I had been the person living with severe foot pain and suicidal distress. Yet I did not know, while I was living through that crisis, how the concern had been framed in the record, what had been attempted on my behalf, or how little appears to have followed from that attempt.

That discovery matters because it shows the danger of a record the patient cannot see while it is governing him.

It also matters because it explains why trust lasted as long as it did. I continued to trust the system partly because I did not know what the system already knew, what had been escalated, or what had not moved. I did not know that the record contained material that would later make the absence of effective intervention difficult to understand.

Then there is the earlier absence.

There are two absences in the record that should be named at the outset.

The first is visual. There is no picture of my feet.[SN11] At no stage in the whole course of treatment was an image of my feet created or preserved in the record.

The second is verbal. On the day before discharge, I made a protest.[SN12] I knew I was leaving hospital with a lifelong diagnosis. But I also knew that the reason I had gone to my GP, and the reason I had come into hospital, was that I had sore feet. Nobody had looked at my feet. I asked about this before discharge.

That protest is not recorded.

This book does not depend on that omission. The thrust of the case rests on what is recorded. But the reader should understand what the omission does. It means that a concern raised before discharge appears nowhere in the official sequence. It means that later concern can be made to look retrospective when, from my perspective, it was present before finality attached. It means that the record is complete only in its own terms.

It contains the documentary basis of this case, but it does not contain everything the patient knew at the time.

The complaint process then changed the situation again.

When I complained in 2025, I was not asking the institution to accept every conclusion I might draw. I was asking it to look again. I was asking it to recognise that a later medication response, severe unresolved foot pain, and the documentary sequence raised questions that could not safely be closed by treating the matter as ordinary disagreement with clinical judgment.

The response made clear to me that the difficulty was not only historical. It was prospective. It concerned access to accurate care.

If the institution's position remained that there was nothing requiring meaningful correction, then the same record that had governed the past would continue to govern the future. The issue was no longer only what had happened in 2017. It was whether I could safely receive care in 2025 while the governing record remained under dispute.

That is why the April 20 submission matters to me.

By then, I was not merely recounting harm. I was telling the institution that I could see the structure forming. I was telling it that the original clinical harm had been compounded by procedural closure. I was asking for a human route, an intermediary, a way for the institution to remain in contact with the reality of the situation before it hardened further. I was not asking for escalation as a tactic. I was asking because I could no longer see another safe pathway.

That request was not outside the case. It is part of the case.

It shows that the patient was not trying to ambush the institution with hindsight. He was telling the institution, while the matter was still live, that the way the record was being handled had itself become dangerous.

The same is true of the later primary-care difficulty.

After the complaint response, and after the record had been formally challenged, I found myself in a position where ordinary care was constrained by the very diagnosis and hierarchy under dispute. My GP could respond humanly. The practice could assist where the issue did not require neurological authority. But when the problem touched Sinemet, dystonia, Botox, levodopa adjustment, or the relation between foot pain and the neurological frame, primary care could not safely govern the matter without specialist input.

That left me in an impossible position.

The disputed record continued to govern. The specialist pathway did not provide the necessary correction. The GP could not step outside the specialist frame. The patient was left managing medication, risk, pain, and interpretation in the space between them.

That is not merely a complaint about inconvenience. It is the practical consequence of an uncorrected record.

The data and access correspondence belongs in the same sequence. It is not a separate technical dispute. If the record governs care, then access to the record, correction of the record, restriction of reliance, and disclosure of process material become part of the patient's route to safety. When the patient seeks documentation about how a crucial interaction was recorded or governed, and the institution says no such personal data is held, that response has practical significance. It does not merely answer an access request. It affects the patient's ability to test the record that governs him.

This is why I am taking the case.

Not because I believe every answer is already known.

Not because I deny that medicine is difficult.

Not because I say that clinicians must have known in 2017 what could only be confirmed later.

Not because I want the past erased.

Not because I think a patient's belief automatically outranks clinical judgment.

I am taking the case because the documents now make the question unavoidable.

The record did not merely describe me. It governed me. It governed how my symptoms were understood, how later facts were interpreted, how my complaint was handled, how my access to care developed, and how difficult it became to return to the original problem. Once that became clear, doing nothing would not be neutral. It would permit the same uncorrected structure to continue.

That conclusion has not come quickly.

As I write this, it is 5 July 2026. My last appointment with the neurologist was on 10 March 2025. I submitted my complaint on 19 March 2025. I later received the response from the neurologist and the Quality and Patient Safety Department in April 2025, although the neurologist's response itself was dated 25 March 2025.

Since then, I have not experienced the available channels as channels of correction.

I have received a departmental response distancing the head of department from the case, even where his name appeared within the material. I have had the matter considered through professional-regulatory channels and brought to no corrective outcome from the patient's perspective. I have had correspondence, referral, complaint, data-protection, and access routes pursued over many months. I have invoked GDPR rights, including restriction, in circumstances where continued reliance on the disputed record was itself part of the problem. I have written to people who had previously known me, supported me, or appeared available as human points of contact. Some did not answer.

That silence matters, but it should not be overstated.

This book does not ask the reader to infer bad faith from silence. It does not ask the reader to convert disappointment into proof. It does not ask the reader to treat every unavailable person, office, or organisation as legally responsible for the same thing.

The point is narrower.

The routes that should have made correction possible were not, in practice, available to me as routes of correction. Clinical review did not reopen the frame. Complaint handling did not restore a safe route through the record. Professional review did not resolve the patient's need for a correctable record. Data rights did not prevent continued reliance in the way I had sought. Primary care remained constrained by the specialist hierarchy. People I had understood as part of my wider support world did not become a bridge back into care.

That is part of why the case has been forced upon me.

Not because I have no choice.

Because the usual alternatives to taking a case had, one by one, failed to provide a route by which the record could be corrected, qualified, restricted, contextualised, or made safe for future reliance.

There is also a personal dimension to that failure, and it should be named carefully.

Before this sequence, I had not understood myself to be outside the care community around Parkinson's disease. I had participated in it. I had raised money for it. I had trusted that the relationships and associations formed around that world would, at the very least, leave a human route open if my own care became unsafe or disputed.

That is not what happened.

The absence of contact from people and organisations I had previously trusted does not prove the clinical case. It does not decide the legal case. But it belongs to the human context in which the case is now being taken. It shows what it means for a patient to discover that the record is not only a clinical file. It can become the thing around which people, offices, systems, and support structures organise their distance.

By that point, the patient is not merely trying to recover from illness.

He is trying to recover from the loss of the routes by which illness, error, uncertainty, and correction are supposed to be held safely.

That is the narrow sense in which the case has been forced upon me.

I remain a person with choice. But I did not choose the conditions that made this choice necessary. I did not choose to be governed by a record I did not control. I did not choose for the founding problem to remain unresolved while finality attached around it. I did not choose for later correction signals to be absorbed into the same frame. I did not choose to discover, only afterwards, that crisis signals, protests, omissions, complaint closure, care-access constraints, and data refusals all belonged to the same sequence.

Once I did discover that, I had to decide what silence would mean.

This book is the result of that decision.

It is not an act of revenge. It is not an attempt to convert suffering into proof. It is not a demand that the reader accept my conclusions because I suffered. It is an attempt to place the documents in order and ask whether the record remained correctable when correction mattered.

The reader should therefore understand the emotional position, but should not decide the case emotionally.

The humane fact is that I trusted the system.

The evidential fact is that the documents must now be read.

The governance question is whether the record was allowed to acquire durable reliance while preserving reasons why that reliance should have remained corrigible.

The personal question is what a patient is supposed to do once he sees that, if he does nothing, the record will continue to stand as though the question has already been answered.

This is my medical experience.

It is part of my history.

It is not my story.

But it is now a record I have to answer.

2. Reader's Note: How to Use This Book

This is a short book about a difficult record.

It is not a pleading. It is not legal advice. It is not medical advice. It is not an expert report. It does not ask the reader to decide liability, diagnosis, causation, professional discipline, or damages. Those are matters for properly instructed lawyers, clinicians, experts, regulators, and decision-makers.

Its purpose is narrower.

This book is a guide to sequence. It asks the reader to follow how a medical problem entered the record, how it was described, how uncertainty was preserved, how finality nevertheless attached, and how later facts were handled when they appeared to disturb the settled frame.

The central issue is corrigibility and reliance.

Corrigibility means the capacity of a record or institutional position to be corrected, qualified, reopened, supplemented, restricted, or contextualised when later information makes continued reliance unsafe.

Reliance means the practical dependence placed on a record by clinicians, administrators, statutory schemes, complaints handlers, data controllers, and the patient himself.

A record can be accurate in parts and still become misleading in operation. It may contain careful observations, sympathetic language, uncertainty, and qualifications. But if those qualifications do not affect what the institution does next, they may lose operative force. They remain in the file, but they do not govern the file.

That is the distinction this book asks the reader to keep in mind.

Documentation is not the same as correction. Mentioning a symptom is not the same as explaining it. Recording a patient's concern is not the same as allowing it to reorganise the clinical frame. Naming uncertainty is not the same as preserving a route back from finality.

The book therefore proceeds in layers.

First, it introduces the governing concepts: corrigibility, reliance, provisional framing, operative authority, and documentary finality.

Second, it follows the record sequence: referral, admission, discharge, certification, treatment decisions, later symptoms, complaint handling, and data issues.

Third, it identifies legal and governance significance without turning those issues into conclusions.

Fourth, it states the cautions. The book must not overclaim. It must not allege malice where the documents do not independently support it. It must not say the patient was simply ignored. It must not project later genetic confirmation backwards as though it had been known in 2017. It must not demand erasure where correction, qualification, supplementation, restriction, or contextualisation is the more coherent remedy.

The documents should be allowed to speak. But documents do not speak by volume alone. They speak by order, contrast, omission, repetition, and use.

A reader should therefore ask not only what a document says, but what the document was allowed to do.

Did it keep the question open?

Did it close the question?

Did it record uncertainty while allowing reliance to harden?

Did it preserve a meaningful route back to correction?

Those questions are the method of the book.

The style is deliberately compact. The aim is not to overwhelm the reader with every available detail. The aim is to make the structure legible enough that the full record can then be read with discipline.

The book should be read as a field guide to a record: a way of seeing what is already there.

3. The Case in One Page

The case is not that a doctor chose one diagnostic label and later events supplied a better one.

That would be too small.

The case is that a healthcare institution recorded uncertainty, atypicality, unresolved features, patient concern, treatment caution, and later contradiction, but still allowed a provisional diagnostic frame to acquire durable authority. That authority became clinical, administrative, statutory, and interpretive. It shaped what later facts were allowed to mean.

The institution did not merely make a diagnosis. It allowed a provisional diagnostic frame to acquire durable reliance while the record itself preserved reasons why that reliance should have remained corrigible.

That is the centre of this book.

A patient entered the hospital pathway through an open problem. The problem was not abstract. It was bodily, practical, and visible. His gait was abnormal. His movement was stiff. His tone was increased. His hands were affected. His work was threatened. His feet were central to the way the problem presented. The referral did not deliver a completed diagnosis. It asked whether the problem was parkinsonian or whether some other underlying diagnosis was responsible.

That distinction matters. A referral of that kind does not hand the receiving institution a closed conclusion. It hands it a question.

The record that followed did identify Parkinsonian features. This book does not deny that. It does not pretend the diagnosis came from nowhere. It does not require the reader to erase bradykinesia, rigidity, abnormal gait, dopamine pathway imaging, or the clinical judgment that Parkinson's disease or young-onset Parkinsonism was a plausible explanation.

The problem is not plausibility.

The problem is finality.

A plausible diagnostic frame can become unsafe if it is allowed to govern before the originating problem has been resolved, falsified, or preserved as unresolved. The issue is not that uncertainty existed. Medicine often begins in uncertainty. The issue is that uncertainty was recorded but not kept operative.

The discharge record is therefore the hinge. It does two things at once.

On one side, it records features that should have restrained closure: young age, atypicality, very little tremor, symmetry, a gait described in dystonic terms, possible autosomal-recessive or genetic Parkinsonism, unresolved leg and foot symptoms, and caution about levodopa or dopamine agonist treatment.

On the other side, it declares a primary diagnosis, initiates a Parkinson's pathway, supports statutory and administrative reliance, and gives the record a settled centre of gravity.

The difficulty is not that the record contains no uncertainty. It does.

The difficulty is that the uncertainty does not govern what happens next.

From that point forward, later events are not treated as reasons to reopen the original question. They are interpreted through the established frame. Foot pain returns. Foot phenomena recur. Medication effects appear. The patient continues to describe a mismatch between the lived problem and the settled explanation. The record acknowledges much of this. It does not simply fall silent. But acknowledgement is not correction.

That is why the phrase "the patient was ignored" is not precise enough.

He was not simply ignored. He was documented. He was described. He was counselled. His intelligence was recognised. His symptoms were written down. His later suffering was acknowledged. His medication response was eventually accepted.

The stronger and more exact formulation is this:

He was documented without governing authority.

His account could enter the record, but it did not reorganise the record. His symptoms could be mentioned, but they did not force the diagnostic frame back into provisionality. His protest could exist in the room, but not in the file. His feet could be the reason he sought help, but never become the controlling object of inquiry.

That is the structure to be tested.

The case is not an invitation to anger in place of evidence. It is the opposite. It depends on the documents. It asks the reader to notice the difference between a record that is detailed and a record that is correctable. It asks whether a patient can be heavily documented and still be deprived of a practical route back to correction.

Later genetic confirmation matters, but it must be handled carefully. It is not used here as a crude retrospective weapon. The book does not argue that a later PRKN/PARK2 result should simply be projected backwards into 2017 as though it had then been known. The point is narrower: the record itself had already placed atypicality, genetic suspicion, and unresolved features on the table. Later confirmation matters because it gives retrospective importance to a line of inquiry already visible in the record. It does not create the earlier uncertainty. It illuminates it.

The same caution applies to treatment response. The fact that Sinemet later relieved the patient's foot experience does not, by itself, decide the whole case. It does not prove every earlier decision wrong. It does not replace expert evidence. But it does matter as a reopening signal, particularly where the original record had deferred levodopa-type treatment while allowing the Parkinsonian frame to become durable.

This is why counsel review is needed.

The legal questions are not simple. They may involve clinical negligence, informed consent, scope of expertise, referral duties, data accuracy, complaint handling, administrative fairness, limitation, causation, and loss. Different routes ask different questions. None should be collapsed into a single grievance.

But beneath those routes lies one governing structure.

Was the record allowed to become final before finality was earned?

Was uncertainty documented but deprived of operative force?

Were later reopening signals absorbed into the existing frame rather than used to correct, qualify, supplement, restrict, or reopen it?

Did the patient inherit a lifelong record that preserved reasons for doubt while denying him a meaningful route back through that doubt?

This book does not answer every legal question. It prepares the reader to ask the right ones.

It is about a correctable record.

It is about what happens when a medical institution records enough to know that a frame should remain open, but allows reliance to harden around that frame anyway.

4. Why "Misdiagnosis" Is Too Small

The easiest way to misunderstand this case is to call it a misdiagnosis case and stop there.

That word is familiar. It is available. It gives the mind a ready-made path: a patient had one condition, a doctor named another, the truth emerged later, and the earlier label must now be judged against the later truth.

That is not the structure of this book.

The problem with the word "misdiagnosis" is not that it is always wrong. It may become relevant. Counsel may need to test it. Experts may need to address it. A court or regulator may eventually have to ask whether a diagnosis was reasonable, unreasonable, premature, incomplete, or misleading.

But as an organising frame, misdiagnosis is too narrow.

It asks the reader to look for the wrong label. This case asks the reader to look at what happened to the record once a label became powerful.

A diagnosis is not merely a word. In a medical system, it can become an organising instrument. It shapes medication choices, follow-up pathways, referral decisions, statutory forms, patient understanding, employment implications, complaint responses, data processing, and the meaning later clinicians attach to symptoms.

When that happens, the issue is no longer only whether the label was right. The issue is whether the label was allowed to govern before it had earned that authority.

This matters because the record here was not simple. It did not say, in one clean line, that all uncertainty had ended. It preserved complexity. It recorded atypicality. It named possible genetic disease. It described a gait in dystonic terms. It recorded very young onset and very little tremor. It acknowledged unresolved leg and foot symptoms. It deferred ordinary Parkinson's treatments because of age and risk. It later recorded further foot phenomena, severe pain, medication response, and patient disagreement with the explanatory frame.

A simple misdiagnosis story tends to flatten those features into one question: was the original diagnosis wrong?

The better question is more exact: what force did those features have inside the institution?

Were they allowed to keep the frame provisional?

Were they treated as reasons for escalation, qualification, or referral?

Were they visible but inert?

Did they survive as words while finality proceeded around them?

This is why the book uses the terms corrigibility and reliance.

Corrigibility asks whether the record remained practically correctable. Not theoretically correctable in the abstract. Practically correctable. Could the patient or a later clinician return to the unresolved features and reopen the governing frame? Could the record accept a qualification without treating it as a mere disagreement? Could later contradiction do more than become another fact absorbed into the existing explanation?

Reliance asks what the record was allowed to do. Once a diagnosis is relied upon by discharge documentation, statutory certification, long-term treatment, complaint handling, and data governance, it acquires weight. It becomes more than an opinion at a point in time. It becomes the structure through which other people act.

The case therefore concerns the relationship between uncertainty and reliance.

A provisional frame can be legitimate at the moment it is formed. It can be clinically plausible. It can even be the best available explanation at the time. But if the same frame is then used as though it has settled the case, while the record still contains reasons why settlement is unsafe, a governance problem arises.

That governance problem is not solved by saying, "the uncertainty was documented."

Documentation is not the same as corrigibility.

A note can say "atypical" and still permit a pathway to become fixed. A letter can say "genetic possibility" and still defer the route that would test it. A discharge summary can say foot and leg symptoms may be separate and still place Parkinson's disease at the centre of the record. A clinician can say the patient may one day regret not starting treatment earlier and still allow the decision not to test that treatment response to coexist with durable diagnostic reliance.

In such a case, the record may be accurate as archive but misleading as governance.

That distinction is central.

An archive preserves what was said. Governance determines what matters.

The patient does not only live with the archive. He lives under the governance created by the archive.

That is why this book does not ask for the old record to be erased. Erasure would be the wrong target. Historical records should usually remain historically intelligible. The more coherent question is whether they need correction, qualification, supplementation, restriction, or contextualisation so that future reliance does not treat provisional or incomplete material as settled.

This is also why later evidence must be handled with discipline.

The later PRKN/PARK2 genetic confirmation matters. It matters because the earlier record already contained genetic suspicion. It matters because the possibility was not invented after the event. It matters because later confirmation can show that a named line of inquiry was more important than the settled frame allowed it to become.

But it should not be used crudely.

The book should not say that later genetic knowledge must simply be projected backwards into 2017. It should not pretend that every later fact was knowable from the beginning. It should not replace legal or medical analysis with hindsight.

The proper use of later contradiction is different. Later contradiction tests the system's openness. It asks whether the institution had preserved a route by which new information could qualify old reliance.

The same is true of medication response. If a later medication effect appears to disturb an earlier explanatory separation between Parkinson's and foot pain, the question is not simply, "why was this not known from day one?" The better question is: once this response became known, did it trigger correction, qualification, or reopening? Or was it accepted while the underlying frame remained intact?

That is the movement from misdiagnosis to corrigibility.

Misdiagnosis is about whether the right name was used.

Corrigibility is about whether the record could recover when its governing name became unsafe, incomplete, or misleading.

This book begins from the second question because it is the stronger and more document-faithful one. It does not require accusing every clinician of wrongdoing. It does not require alleging malice. It does not require denying that Parkinsonian features existed. It does not require pretending that medicine is easy or that diagnosis is mechanical.

It requires only that finality be earned.

Where a record contains uncertainty, atypicality, unresolved features, and later contradiction, an institution should not allow a provisional frame to become durable without preserving a meaningful route back to correction.

That is why "misdiagnosis" is too small.

The case is not only about the label.

It is about the authority the label acquired.

5. Documentation Is not Corrigibility

A medical record can be long and still not be correctable.

That is one of the central lessons of this case.

The problem is not that nothing was written down. Much was written down. The record contains descriptions, impressions, explanations, letters, plans, medication discussions, risk discussions, patient correspondence, complaint responses, and later reflections. It is not an empty record. It is not a silent record. It is not a record from which the patient has simply vanished.

That makes the problem more difficult, but also more important.

Because the question is not whether the patient appears in the documents.

The question is what authority his appearance has.

There is a difference between a fact being recorded and a fact being allowed to govern. A symptom can be mentioned without becoming central. A concern can be transcribed without altering the route of inquiry. A qualification can be inserted while the document as a whole performs closure. A doubt can be preserved as language but denied practical consequence.

That is the difference between documentation and corrigibility.

Documentation means the fact exists somewhere in the file.

Corrigibility means the fact can change what the file does.

A record is corrigible when its own uncertainties remain capable of reopening the frame. It is corrigible when a later clinician, complaints officer, data controller, or lawyer can look back and see not only what was concluded, but what remained unsettled and what should therefore qualify continued reliance. It is corrigible when later contradiction can attach to an earlier uncertainty and move the institution from defence to reconsideration.

A record is not corrigible merely because it contains many words.

It is not corrigible merely because a clinician was careful to describe complexity.

It is not corrigible merely because a patient was copied into letters or spoken to at length.

It is not corrigible merely because one line says "atypical" while another line proceeds as though the case has been settled.

This distinction is easy to miss because documentation can look like fairness. It can feel like recognition. It can give the appearance that the patient's reality has been taken into account. In many cases, documentation is important and protective. A sparse record may hide the very facts needed for review. But a detailed record can produce a different danger. It can preserve uncertainty while arranging the document so that uncertainty has no operative force.

Operative force means the capacity to affect what happens next.

If a record says the gait is unusual but no route follows from that unusualness, the observation may lack operative force.

If a record says genetic disease is plausible but the diagnostic frame is allowed to become durable while genetic clarification is deferred, the genetic possibility may lack operative force.

If a record says foot and leg symptoms may be separate, but the patient leaves with a lifelong primary diagnosis and no completed explanation of the feet, the separation may lack operative force.

If a patient protests that the reason for attendance has not been addressed, but the protest does not enter the record, it has no documentary force at all.

This is why the sentence "he was ignored" is too crude.

It is not careful enough for this case. It can be answered too easily by pointing to the length of the record, the correspondence, the consultations, the explanations, and the patient's presence in the documents.

The more exact formulation is stronger:

He was documented without governing authority.

That phrase should be read slowly.

"Documented" means he was present in the record. His body, his symptoms, his history, his intelligence, his questions, and his distress were all, at various points, recognised.

"Without governing authority" means that his account did not control the hierarchy of explanation. His feet could be described, but they did not become the organising problem. His pain could be acknowledged, but it did not force the diagnostic frame back into provisional status. His later improvement with medication could be accepted, but it did not necessarily require the institution to revisit the earlier separation between Parkinsonian disease and foot pain. His protest could exist as lived fact, but without being recorded it could not govern the official sequence.

That is a specific kind of institutional failure. It is not the failure to notice a human being at all. It is the failure to let the documented human being alter the governing frame.

The same point applies to uncertainty.

The record did not necessarily remove uncertainty. It contained it. But containing uncertainty is not the same as keeping uncertainty alive.

Uncertainty remains alive only if it has consequences. It must affect diagnosis, referral, consent, treatment options, escalation, qualification, or later correction. It must warn future readers that reliance should be cautious. It must prevent provisional conclusions from becoming more settled than the evidence permits.

If uncertainty is merely archived, it can become decorative. It shows that complexity was seen, but not that complexity governed.

This matters because later review often treats documentation as an answer. A complaint may be closed because the relevant issues were "documented." A data request may be resisted because the historical record accurately records what clinicians thought at the time. A clinical concern may be framed as disagreement because the record already contains the clinician's explanation. A governance issue may be reduced to the existence of notes.

But the existence of notes does not answer the deeper question.

What did the notes permit?

What did they prevent?

What did they make durable?

What did they leave correctable?

A record can be historically accurate and operationally misleading. That is not a contradiction. A record may accurately show that a clinician held a view on a given date. It may accurately preserve the pathway chosen at that time. But if later reliance treats that historical view as settled truth, without preserving the qualifications that made it provisional, the record can mislead in use.

This is why correction does not necessarily mean erasure.

The patient need not demand that the old documents disappear. The old documents are part of the sequence. They show what happened. They show what was thought. They show what was not resolved. They show where uncertainty sat beside finality.

The remedy that fits this kind of problem is different. It may be correction. It may be qualification. It may be supplementation. It may be contextualisation. It may be restriction of reliance. It may be a clear note that certain historical entries should not be read without later developments and unresolved features.

The aim is not to rewrite history.

The aim is to prevent history from governing falsely.

This is the core of corrigibility. A correctable record is one that can carry its own history without becoming trapped by it. It can say, "this was the view then," while also saying, "continued reliance on that view now requires qualification." It can preserve the past without making the past immune from later correction.

That is the kind of record a patient should be able to rely on.

The patient relies on the documents too. They are not only instruments of institutional defence. They are the patient's map back through the medical experience. If the documents record him but do not let his recorded reality govern, then the patient is placed in a difficult position. The very record he must rely upon to prove what happened may also be the mechanism by which what happened is contained.

That is why documentation is not enough.

Documentation is the beginning of accountability.

Corrigibility is what prevents documentation from becoming a locked room.

6. The Open Referral

The sequence begins before the diagnosis.

That matters.

A case should not be read only from the point at which a label appears. It should be read from the point at which the institution accepts a problem for explanation. The first question is not, "what diagnosis was later written?" The first question is, "what problem entered the system?"

Here, the problem entered through the feet, the gait, the body, and the loss of ordinary function.

The referral was not a neat request to confirm an already-settled disease. It was an open referral.[SN1] It described deterioration. It described an unusual gait. It described stiffness. It described trouble initiating movement. It described increased tone. It described brisk reflexes. It described difficulty with dexterity and work. It described feet that were visibly and practically part of the problem.

The feet were not decorative detail.

They were part of the presenting route into the hospital pathway.

The patient had bilateral foot issues. He had corns and callouses. He had pain and abnormal walking. His feet were described as not being in a good state. He had difficulty functioning at work. His gait was unusual enough to trouble the general practitioner. The referral raised the possibility of a parkinsonian condition, but it did not close the question. It also allowed for some other underlying diagnosis.

That openness is important because it fixes the starting position.

The hospital was not handed a finished conclusion. It was handed a problem requiring explanation.

The case therefore begins with diagnostic duty in a broad sense. Not a duty, in this book, already translated into a legal finding. That is for counsel and experts. But as a matter of record sequence, the institution accepted an unresolved presentation. The patient's feet, gait, stiffness, and function were the reason the matter came forward.

The later record cannot fairly be read as though the feet were a side issue that appeared only after the diagnosis. They were there at the beginning.

This is why the referral matters more than its length might suggest. It supplies the entry conditions. It shows the reader what had to be accounted for before finality could safely attach. If a patient enters care because his gait is failing and his feet are central to the visible problem, a later record should either explain that foot-centred presentation, rule it out as governing, or preserve it as unresolved.

It should not simply outgrow it.

A diagnostic frame may become plausible very quickly. That is not itself a problem. A clinician seeing rigidity, bradykinesia, abnormal gait, and difficulty initiating movement may reasonably consider Parkinsonism. The referral itself made that possibility explicit. But a possibility named at referral is not the same as a conclusion earned at discharge.

The word "parkinsonian" is not the end of the inquiry. It is an opening into one possible pathway.

That distinction is central to the whole case. The issue is not whether Parkinsonian features existed. The issue is whether the presence of Parkinsonian features allowed the system to stop being answerable to the rest of the presentation.

The referral did not ask only for a name. It asked for assessment of a person whose functioning was collapsing.

The feet matter because they make the case concrete. They resist abstraction. A diagnostic label can become very large, very quickly. It can explain movement, stiffness, gait, tremor, medication, prognosis, benefits, referrals, and future expectations. But a pair of painful feet has a stubborn simplicity. The patient went to the GP because walking hurt and function was failing. He came into hospital because the problem had become serious. Before the institution could safely close around a lifelong diagnostic frame, that problem required a governing explanation.

Not necessarily the explanation the patient preferred.

Not necessarily the explanation that would later be proved genetically or pharmacologically.

But some accountable explanation.

The referral therefore creates a discipline for the reader. Whenever later documents become complex, the reader should return to the opening question: what happened to the problem that brought him there?

Did the record explain the feet?

Did it examine them as a primary object?

Did it show why they were no longer central?

Did it preserve the possibility that they remained unresolved?

Did the later diagnosis answer the question, or merely stand above it?

This is not a sentimental point. It is evidential. The starting point controls the sequence. If the initial problem is lost, the record can appear more settled than it was. The diagnosis can become the centre, while the reason for attendance becomes peripheral.

That is one way a record can become operationally misleading.

nothing need be falsified. The notes may accurately record observations and impressions. But if the original problem loses status without being resolved, the record begins to reorganise the case around its conclusion rather than its question.

The open referral also matters because the patient was not medically sophisticated enough to name the problem in technical terms. He did not need to be. Patients bring problems before they bring diagnoses. They say what is happening to them: walking is wrong, feet hurt, work is becoming impossible, movement is stiff, life is narrowing. The institution's task is to translate that account into medical inquiry without losing the account itself.

That is the point at which patient testimony first risks becoming subordinate.

The patient may say, in plain terms, "my feet are the problem." The system may record foot issues. It may then proceed to a neurological frame that treats the feet as secondary, separate, peripheral, podiatric, neuropathic, or merely part of a larger Parkinsonian condition. Any of those explanations may be clinically possible. But the record must show how the move was made.

A reader should be cautious about treating a later specialist frame as though it automatically answers the lay presenting problem. It may answer it. It may not. The document must be read to see whether the connection was made or assumed.

In this case, the referral left the connection open.

That openness is the first protection the record should have preserved.

The patient did not enter the system asking for a lifelong label. He entered it because something was wrong with his walking, his feet, his body, and his capacity to work. The referral asked for help in explaining that deterioration. It did not ask the hospital to produce a frame that would become durable while the feet remained unresolved.

For that reason, the referral is not merely background.

It is the first constraint on finality.

Everything that follows should be read against it.

7. The Discharge Hinge

The discharge document is the hinge of the case.

It is the place where uncertainty and finality appear together.

That is why it must be read slowly: not as a bad document, not as a careless document, and not as a document that says only one thing. Its importance lies in the fact that it says more than one thing at the same time.

On one side, the discharge document records complexity. It records a young patient. It records Parkinsonian features. It records atypicality. It records very little tremor. It records marked tone. It records a gait described in dystonic terms. It records unresolved foot and leg symptoms. It records possible genetic disease. It records uncertainty about whether some symptoms were part of the Parkinsonian picture, separate from it, or related to another explanation.

On the other side, it performs closure. It names Parkinson's disease as the primary diagnosis.[SN2] It places the patient on a Parkinson's pathway. It sets out treatment and follow-up. It supports administrative and statutory reliance. It leaves the patient with a lifelong diagnostic frame.

The problem is not that either side is hidden.

The problem is that they coexist.

A discharge document has a different force from a working note. It is not just a memory aid. It travels. It summarises. It instructs. It tells the GP what has happened. It tells future clinicians where the centre of gravity lies. It tells the patient what the institution has decided. It can become the document from which later care, medication, entitlement, complaint handling, and institutional position all begin.

That is why finality in a discharge document matters.

A discharge summary can preserve uncertainty in its paragraphs while still creating practical finality through its structure. The heading, diagnosis field, medication list, follow-up plan, and administrative acts may speak louder than the caveats embedded below them. A reader may notice the caveats, but the system may rely on the diagnosis.

That is the hinge.

The document does not merely describe a clinical impression. It changes the patient's administrative reality. It makes the provisional frame usable by others.

This matters especially where the unresolved feature is the same feature that brought the patient into the system. The patient came through an open foot-centred gait problem. The discharge document did not erase the feet. It discussed them. But it did not make them governing. It separated, qualified, or subordinated them while allowing Parkinson's disease to become the organising diagnosis.

There is a crucial difference between saying "the feet are mentioned" and saying "the feet are explained."

The feet can appear in the discharge document and still remain unresolved. They can be acknowledged as painful, calloused, mechanically affected, burning, separate, secondary, neuropathic, podiatric, or part of a wider condition. But unless the document shows why the original foot-centred problem no longer controls the inquiry, the mention is not enough.

The discharge document also contains the treatment hinge.

Levodopa and dopamine agonists were ordinary Parkinson's treatments. The record recognised their role. It also recorded reasons for deferring them in a young patient, including later treatment complications. That caution may have been clinically understandable. This book does not treat caution itself as negligence.

The question is what followed from the caution.

If a principal symptomatic treatment is deferred, and if that treatment might also have helped clarify the relationship between movement, pain, dystonia, and the feet, then the record needed a way to preserve provisionality. The institution could say: we are not starting this yet, for risk reasons; therefore the diagnostic and explanatory frame remains qualified. Or it could escalate, refer, test, or mark the unresolved features more clearly for later review.

What creates the governance problem is not simply the decision to defer.

It is the combination of deferral and durable reliance.

The same document can say treatment is being withheld for caution while also allowing the diagnosis to become strong enough to govern. That combination matters. If the treatment route that might later operate as a discriminator is postponed, then the diagnostic frame should not become more final than the remaining inquiry permits.

Genetics is another hinge.

The document did not lack genetic awareness. Possible inherited or recessive Parkinsonian disease was contemplated.[SN5] That matters because later genetic confirmation did not arrive from nowhere. It returned to a path already visible in the record. The issue is not that clinicians in 2017 had to possess later results. The issue is that the record already contained enough genetic suspicion to make premature administrative settlement unsafe unless the unresolved genetic question was preserved with force.

A scope limitation also matters.

Where a clinician recognises the complexity of a movement-disorders presentation and acknowledges limits of specialist scope, that acknowledgement should have consequences. It may call for referral, supervision, escalation, or express qualification. A disclaimer is not the same as a route to correction. If the case is complex enough to acknowledge limits, it may also be complex enough to resist durable finality.

The discharge document therefore raises a sequence question.

What had been resolved before finality attached?

Had the feet been examined as the originating problem?

Had the dystonic-looking gait been explained?

Had the genetic possibility been pursued or preserved as materially open?

Had levodopa-type treatment been tested, or had its deferral been paired with sufficient provisionality?

Had the patient been left with a record that made clear what remained unresolved?

These are not rhetorical questions. They are counsel questions. They require legal and expert testing. But the discharge document is where those questions become visible.

It is important not to overstate the point. The discharge document is not being treated here as proof of malice. It is not being treated as proof that no care occurred. It is not being treated as proof that Parkinson's disease was an irrational consideration. The document itself is too complex for that kind of simplicity.

The stronger point is more disciplined.

The discharge document is an artefact of simultaneous uncertainty and finality.

It records reasons for caution while also enabling reliance. It preserves unresolved features while also creating a durable diagnostic centre. It acknowledges complexity while making the record administratively usable as though the case has been sufficiently settled.

That is why this document is the hinge.

After discharge, later facts no longer encounter an open field. They encounter a frame. The frame has a name. It has a pathway. It has medication logic. It has statutory consequences. It has explanatory authority. Later foot pain, medication response, patient protest, and genetic information must now fight their way back through that authority.

That is very different from being evaluated before finality attaches.

In an open sequence, new facts can govern the direction of inquiry.

In a closed sequence, new facts are first asked whether they can be made compatible with the existing frame.

The discharge document is the moment where that change begins.

It is the hinge between inquiry and reliance.

It is the point at which a provisional frame starts to acquire the force of a settled record.

8. How Uncertainty Lost Operative Force

Uncertainty can survive in a record and still lose power.

That is the governing paradox of this case.

The documents did not necessarily pretend that everything was simple. They did not strip away every qualification. They did not remove every sign of complexity. On the contrary, the record contains many features that should make a careful reader pause: atypicality, young age, limited tremor, dystonic-looking gait, possible genetic Parkinsonism, unresolved foot and leg symptoms, treatment caution, and later contradiction.

But the presence of uncertainty is not enough.

The question is what the uncertainty was allowed to do.

Operative force means practical effect. A fact has operative force when it changes conduct, limits reliance, triggers escalation, preserves provisionality, invites correction, or prevents premature closure. It does not merely appear. It acts.

A fact without operative force may still be visible. It may sit in a paragraph, in a letter, in a clinic note, or in a complaint response. It may even be phrased carefully. But if the institution proceeds as though the fact does not disturb the governing frame, the fact has been documented without being allowed to govern.

That is how uncertainty can lose force.

It is not erased. It is subordinated.

This can happen quietly. No one needs to declare that uncertainty no longer matters. A record may simply arrange itself so that the diagnosis field, pathway, treatment plan, certification, and later explanatory letters do the practical work, while the caveats remain as background. The uncertainty remains available to show that complexity was recognised, but unavailable to prevent reliance from hardening.

That is a subtle failure. It is also a serious one.

A provisional diagnosis should remain provisional in use, not only in language. If a frame is tentative, the documents and decisions that flow from it should carry that tentativeness forward. Later readers should be able to see what was settled, what was not settled, and what would require reopening.

Where that does not happen, provisionality becomes cosmetic.

The record may look cautious, but function as final.

This distinction matters because medical systems rely on records over time. A discharge summary is not read only by the person who wrote it. It becomes part of a chain. Later clinicians inherit it. General practitioners rely on it. Nurses and pharmacists work within it. Forms and schemes translate it into administrative status. Complaints bodies ask whether care departed from it. Data controllers ask whether the file accurately preserves it. The patient himself may be forced to use it as his only map.

Once reliance begins, uncertainty must either travel with the record or be lost.

If uncertainty travels with the record, later readers are warned. They understand that the diagnosis was not a complete answer to all presenting features. They understand that certain symptoms remained unresolved. They understand that later developments may require qualification. They understand that the patient should not be trapped under a conclusion that the record itself had not fully earned.

If uncertainty does not travel with the record, the diagnosis travels alone.

That is when finality becomes dangerous.

Finality is not one thing. It can take several forms.

There is clinical finality: the working diagnosis begins to guide treatment and interpretation.

There is administrative finality: the diagnosis structures appointments, letters, pathways, coding, and institutional handling.

There is statutory finality: the diagnosis is used for entitlement, certification, or formal status.

There is interpretive finality: later facts are understood through the earlier frame before they are allowed to challenge it.

There is complaint finality: the institution treats a challenge to the record as disagreement with clinical judgment rather than a question about whether the record has become misleading in use.

There is data finality: the historical record is defended as accurate because it reflects what was believed at the time, even if continued reliance now requires qualification.

These forms of finality reinforce one another. A diagnosis placed in a discharge document may support a form. The form may confirm the pathway. The pathway may shape treatment. Treatment may shape later interpretation. Later interpretation may support complaint closure. Complaint closure may support data resistance. What began as a working frame becomes a system of reliance.

At that stage, correction becomes harder.

The patient is no longer asking the institution to consider an open question. He is asking it to reopen a settled structure. That is a very different burden.

This is why sequence matters. It is not only what the record contains. It is the order in which the record did things.

Inquiry should precede finality.

If finality comes first, later inquiry must fight uphill. Later facts are no longer free to govern the case. They must first overcome the authority already given to the earlier frame.

That is the core ordering problem.

The record can be detailed and still wrongly ordered. It can say many true things but put them in a hierarchy that makes correction difficult. It can acknowledge uncertainty after giving practical authority to the conclusion. It can preserve caveats while letting reliance proceed as if those caveats were not structurally important.

This is especially important where the unresolved feature is the original presenting problem.

If the patient came through a foot-centred gait failure, then unresolved foot symptoms should have had high operative force. They should have controlled the inquiry until explained, falsified, or clearly preserved as unresolved. They should not have become secondary merely because the Parkinsonian frame had explanatory reach.

A powerful diagnosis can make other facts seem smaller.

That is one way uncertainty loses force. Once the large frame is in place, the smaller facts are made to orbit it. Foot pain becomes podiatry, neuropathy, magnified pain, dystonia, off-time, medication management, or patient concern. Each of these may be possible. But the question is whether the record ever returned to the original problem and asked whether the frame had answered it.

The issue is not whether every later foot symptom had one cause.

The issue is whether the institution preserved a route to ask that question.

A correctable record must leave doors open where the evidence has not closed them. It must not allow uncertainty to become decorative. It must not use detailed documentation as a substitute for operative caution. It must not let a provisional frame become durable simply because it is useful.

Usefulness is not finality.

Plausibility is not finality.

Documentation is not finality.

Only completed, or properly qualified, inquiry can justify finality.

This chapter therefore supplies a test for reading the rest of the book. Whenever an uncertainty appears, ask what happened to it.

Did it change the plan?

Did it limit reliance?

Did it trigger referral?

Did it preserve a warning for future readers?

Did it create a route back?

Or did it remain in the text while the system moved on?

Where the answer is that the system moved on, the uncertainty has lost operative force.

That is the mechanism by which a record can preserve reasons for doubt while still producing durable reliance.

That is the mechanism this book asks counsel to test.

9. The Patient Inside the Record

The patient is not absent from the record.

That is why the case must be stated carefully.

It would be inaccurate, and weaker, to say that he was simply ignored. The documents do not support that simple formulation. He was seen. He was examined. He was described. He was written to. He was copied into correspondence. His intelligence was recognised. His questions were answered. His symptoms were discussed. His later pain was acknowledged. His medication response was eventually accepted.

The record is full of him.

But presence is not authority.

A patient can be extensively present in a record and still not govern the record. His words can be quoted without changing the hierarchy of explanation. His concerns can be acknowledged without altering the diagnostic frame. His symptoms can be described without becoming the organising problem. His distress can be recognised without triggering correction.

This is the distinction the book must hold.

The patient was documented without governing authority.

That phrase does not mean he had no voice. It means his voice did not control the institutional sequence. He could speak into the record, but the record decided what his speech meant. He could describe the feet, but the frame decided whether the feet were neurological, podiatric, neuropathic, dystonic, secondary, separate, or merely part of a wider Parkinsonian condition. He could identify mismatch, but the record could translate mismatch into belief, concern, anxiety, misunderstanding, or difficulty accepting the explanation.

This is not necessarily done with cruelty.

That is important. A system can subordinate a patient without intending to harm him. It can do so through ordinary record forms, specialist language, diagnostic hierarchy, clinical confidence, risk management, complaint categories, and administrative closure. The patient may be treated politely and still lose governing authority. He may be trusted as sincere and still not be trusted as structurally important.

That is why this case should not be written as a story of personal bad faith unless the documents independently support it.

The more exact issue is epistemic placement: where did the patient's account sit inside the record?

Was it central?

Was it explanatory?

Was it capable of reopening the diagnosis?

Or was it material to be interpreted by the existing frame?

The patient entered the system through a practical problem. His walking was wrong. His feet hurt. His work was threatened. His movement had become difficult. Those are not expert conclusions. They are patient facts. They are the kind of facts a person brings before medicine translates them into specialist terms.

The danger begins when translation becomes displacement.

Medicine must translate. That is part of its work. A patient says, "I cannot walk properly." A clinician thinks about tone, bradykinesia, dystonia, neuropathy, musculoskeletal change, medication response, imaging, genetics, and differential diagnosis. That translation can be valuable. It can reveal what the patient cannot name.

But translation must remain answerable to the original account.

If the translated frame no longer explains the patient's problem, or if it explains some features while leaving others unresolved, the patient's account should retain power. It should keep the inquiry open. It should restrain finality. It should make later contradiction meaningful.

Where it does not, the patient is inside the record but below the record.

This is particularly important in a case involving feet.

Feet are not abstract. They are ordinary, visible, stubborn facts. They are the contact point between the body and the world. If walking is failing because the feet hurt or behave abnormally, a patient does not need specialist vocabulary to know that the feet matter. He may be wrong about the cause. But he is not wrong to identify them as the lived site of the problem.

The record's task is not to accept the patient's explanation uncritically.

The task is to account for the patient's problem.

There is a difference.

A patient may say, "my feet are the problem," and the correct medical answer may be complex. It may involve the brain, the nervous system, dystonia, dopamine, peripheral pain, skeletal structure, medication timing, or more than one process. But if the record moves to a higher-level diagnostic label without showing how the feet were accounted for, the patient has been displaced.

His words remain.

His problem loses rank.

The unrecorded protest matters in that light. The day before discharge, the patient says he asked about the feet because he knew he was leaving with a lifelong diagnosis and still knew that the reason he had entered the pathway had not been addressed. That protest is not part of the clinical file. This book does not rely on it as the evidential foundation of the case. The foundation remains the documents. But the absence matters for understanding the record's limits.

The record can only govern through what it contains.

If a protest is not recorded, it cannot operate inside the official sequence. It cannot restrain discharge. It cannot warn future readers. It cannot show that finality was challenged at the time. It cannot become part of the institutional memory.

That is why omission is different from disagreement.

A recorded disagreement can be assessed later. An unrecorded protest must first fight to be believed as having happened at all. The patient is then placed in a double bind: the very file that failed to record the protest is later treated as the authoritative account of whether the protest mattered.

This is another form of lost governing authority.

The patient relies on the documents. The institution relies on the documents. Counsel relies on the documents. Regulators and data controllers rely on the documents. But only the institution controlled what entered them at the crucial time.

That asymmetry should be kept visible.

It does not prove wrongdoing by itself. It does not decide causation. It does not replace clinical evidence. But it affects how the record should be read. A document is not neutral merely because it is detailed. It is an institutional artefact. It reflects what the institution chose, noticed, prioritised, named, omitted, and allowed to matter.

The humane point and the legal point meet here.

A patient should not have to become procedurally expert in order for his basic presenting problem to remain visible. He should not have to know which phrase will trigger a referral, which symptom will preserve a differential, which protest must be documented, or which medication response may later become legally significant. He is entitled to bring the problem as he can name it.

The institution holds the technical language.

Because it holds the technical language, it also carries responsibility for not letting that language bury the patient.

This book therefore keeps returning to one question: did the patient become evidence, or did he remain a source of governance?

Evidence can be filed.

Governance can change direction.

If the patient is treated only as evidence, his symptoms are entered into a frame already chosen. If he remains a source of governance, his account can disturb the frame when the frame fails to account for him.

That is the difference.

The patient inside this record was not a silent figure. He was present. He was legible. He was often carefully described. But the central question is whether his recorded reality had the authority to keep the case open.

The answer to that question is not supplied by the volume of documentation.

It is supplied by the sequence.

10. Reopening Signals

A reopening signal is not proof.

It is a warning that an earlier frame may need to be revisited.

That distinction matters. This chapter does not argue that any single later event decides the whole case. It does not say that every symptom, medication response, or later result proves the original diagnosis was wrong. It does not ask the reader to treat hindsight as evidence of earlier negligence.

A reopening signal does something narrower.

It asks whether the system remained capable of correction.

When a later fact appears to disturb the governing frame, a correctable record should have a way to respond. It may qualify the earlier position. It may refer onward. It may reopen the differential. It may add a caution to the record. It may restrict future reliance. It may explain clearly why the later fact does not alter the earlier frame. What it should not do is simply absorb the fact while leaving the structure of reliance untouched.

This case contains several such signals.

The first type of signal is recurrence.

When a symptom or phenomenon returns in a way that appears linked to a medication change, the record should ask what that recurrence means. It may mean very little. It may mean something important. But if the original unresolved problem was foot-centred, then a returning foot phenomenon should not be treated as incidental without explanation.

The outside-left-foot phenomenon is important in that way. Its return after procyclidine was stopped does not, by itself, solve the case.[SN6] But it should have had reopening force because it linked the feet, movement, and medication in a way that touched the original presentation. It was not a new complaint floating outside the sequence. It belonged to the same bodily territory that had brought the patient into the system.

A correctable record would ask: does this recurrence qualify the earlier separation of foot symptoms from the governing neurological frame?

The second type of signal is unrealised treatment response.

The record contains caution about levodopa and dopamine agonists. That caution may have been clinically understandable in a young patient. The point is not that caution was automatically wrong. The point is that the same record also recognised that such treatment could make the patient much better.

That creates a structural tension.

If a treatment route might substantially change function, and if it is deferred for risk reasons, then the frame should remain qualified. The system should preserve the fact that a significant discriminator or therapeutic test has not yet been used. A decision to defer may be reasonable. But if durable reliance attaches while the principal treatment route is deferred, then the deferral itself becomes a reason to keep the record open.

A correctable record would say: this diagnosis may be plausible, but an important treatment-response question remains untested.

The third type of signal is substitution.

When Sinemet eventually entered the treatment pathway, it did not arrive as a clean, structured return to the original question.[SN7] It entered after years of management, medication balancing, and the removal or replacement of other medication. That matters because the moment at which a medication first becomes unavoidable may not be the same as the moment at which it is used to test the unresolved problem.

A drug can be introduced clinically without reopening the record diagnostically.

That is one of the important distinctions in this case.

If Sinemet begins to change the patient's experience of the feet, the question is not only whether the medication is useful. The question is whether the usefulness should cause the institution to reconsider earlier explanations. Does it qualify the prior treatment narrative? Does it disturb the separation between Parkinsonian movement disorder, foot pain, dystonia, neuropathic pain, and podiatric explanation? Does it require a note that the original foot-centred presentation remained incompletely explained?

A correctable record would not merely adjust the dose. It would ask what the response meant.

The fourth type of signal is crisis.

By 2023, the foot pain had become severe enough to generate urgent concern.[SN8] The patient described extreme bilateral burning pain in both feet and lower legs. The general practitioner became worried about what might happen if the pain continued unaddressed. The language around death wish and urgent review is not incidental. It shows that the unresolved bodily problem had not become historical background. It remained active, severe, and governance-relevant.

A crisis does not prove that the earlier frame was wrong.

But it does test whether the frame is sufficient.

Where a patient continues to suffer severely in the same territory as the original presentation, the record should not treat that suffering as merely another management problem unless the underlying relationship has been explained. The question is not only how to relieve pain. It is whether the persistence and severity of the pain expose a gap in the original diagnostic settlement.

A correctable record would ask: has the founding problem ever been resolved?

The fifth type of signal is recorded misalignment.

Misalignment is a powerful word because it does not require the patient to be proved right on the spot. It simply recognises that there is a gap between the institutional explanation and the patient's experience. In this case, the patient continued to believe that the feet were separate, primary, or insufficiently accounted for. The record recognised that this mismatch existed.

That recognition should matter.

Once misalignment is recorded, the institution has choices. It can treat the misalignment as misunderstanding. It can treat it as disagreement. It can treat it as anxiety. Or it can treat it as evidence that the explanatory frame has not fully accounted for the patient's lived presentation.

A correctable record should at least preserve the latter possibility.

The sixth type of signal is contradiction.

In 2025, the record accepted that Parkinson's medication relieved the patient's experience of pain in the feet[SN9], while also maintaining that the pain was not, in itself, a direct part of Parkinson's disease in the simple way the patient had come to suspect. That tension is subtle but important. A drug may relieve the experience of pain without proving the pain's origin. Parkinson's may magnify pain without causing all pain. Foot pain may be dystonic, neuropathic, mechanical, central, peripheral, or mixed.

But where the medication response cuts across the earlier explanations, the record should not simply hold both propositions without consequence.

Contradiction is not always defeat. Sometimes it is the beginning of better analysis. A correctable system should be able to say: this later response requires us to qualify the earlier certainty, or at least to mark the historical record as incomplete.

The seventh type of signal is genetic confirmation.

This is the signal most likely to be mishandled, so it must be stated with discipline.

The later PRKN/PARK2 confirmation should not be used as a blunt hindsight weapon.[SN10] It should not be written as though clinicians in 2017 possessed knowledge they did not yet possess. It should not turn a governance case into a simple retrospective accusation.

Its significance is narrower and stronger.

The earlier record already contained genetic suspicion. It already named a young, atypical, potentially recessive Parkinsonian presentation. It already contemplated genetic routes. Later confirmation therefore matters because it returns to a path already visible in the record. It shows that a line of uncertainty preserved in the file was not fanciful. It became real.

That does not automatically decide breach.

It does test corrigibility.

A correctable record would be able to absorb genetic confirmation by qualifying the earlier frame, clarifying the historical pathway, and marking what should no longer be relied upon without context. An uncorrectable record may instead treat the later confirmation as merely another detail within the same settled structure.

The eighth type of signal is complaint response.

When a patient raises a structured complaint after a major medication response or later contradiction, the complaint itself can become a reopening signal. It is not only an expression of dissatisfaction. It is a request that the institution reconsider how its record has operated. If that request is treated only as disagreement with clinical judgment, the governance issue may be missed.

A complaint can ask a clinical question.

It can also ask a record question.

Was the file complete enough? Was it qualified enough? Was reliance safe? Did later facts require correction? Did the patient have a meaningful route to have the record revisited?

A correctable system should be able to hear that kind of complaint as a governance issue, not merely as an appeal against a doctor's opinion.

These reopening signals should not be exaggerated. Each requires careful reading. Some may have clinical explanations consistent with the existing frame. Some may not. Counsel and experts must test their meaning.

But their cumulative importance lies in sequence.

The same territory returns.

The feet return.

The medication question returns.

The mismatch returns.

The genetic possibility returns.

The pain returns.

The patient's concern returns.

The record records much of this. But the question is whether the returns had power.

A reopening signal has failed if it is recorded only to be contained.

It has succeeded if it creates a route back into the governing frame.

This chapter therefore marks the turning point of the book. The early chapters ask how finality attached. This chapter asks what happened when later facts should have tested that finality.

The answer is not supplied by any single event.

It lies in the pattern.

If later signals are repeatedly documented but repeatedly absorbed, the problem is not silence. The problem is containment.

That is why the case remains one of corrigibility and reliance.

The issue is not only what the institution knew at the beginning.

It is whether the institution could correct itself when its own record gave it reasons to return.

11. Frame Management

A diagnostic frame does not only name a condition.

It manages meaning.

Once a frame becomes dominant, later facts do not arrive on neutral ground. They arrive inside an interpretive system that has already been built. The frame tells the reader what counts as central, what counts as secondary, what counts as expected, what counts as unrelated, what counts as patient misunderstanding, and what counts as confirmation.

That is why this case cannot be understood only by asking whether Parkinson's disease was a plausible diagnosis. Plausibility is not the end of the matter. A plausible frame can still become too powerful if it governs facts that should have been allowed to challenge it.

Frame management is the process by which later facts are made compatible with the existing frame.

Sometimes that is legitimate. Medicine requires interpretation. A clinician must decide whether a new symptom is part of the known condition, a side effect, a complication, a separate disease, a functional overlay, a pain syndrome, a medication issue, or something else entirely.

But frame management becomes unsafe when the existing diagnosis becomes the condition of admissibility. A fact is then accepted only if it can be translated into the frame. If it cannot be translated, it may be minimised, separated, deferred, or treated as a matter of patient perception.

The fact is not necessarily denied.

It is governed.

In this case, the Parkinsonian frame had explanatory reach. It could explain movement. It could explain stiffness. It could explain gait. It could explain medication decisions. It could explain caution around treatment. It could explain some pain as magnified by Parkinson's disease. It could explain dystonia. It could explain why some therapies were delayed. It could explain why some later options were limited.

That reach made the frame powerful.

The question is whether it became too powerful.

A frame becomes too powerful when it can explain both the presence and the non-operation of contrary facts. If the feet are painful, the frame can say pain is magnified by Parkinson's. If the feet seem separate, the frame can say they may be podiatric or neuropathic. If medication helps, the frame can say Parkinson's medication relieves the experience of pain without proving the pain is directly Parkinsonian. If medication is deferred, the frame can say the risks of early treatment justify delay. If the patient disagrees, the frame can say the patient reasonably perceives misalignment but the diagnosis remains.

Each individual move may have a possible clinical rationale.

The governance issue lies in the pattern.

When every later fact can be absorbed without changing the status of the original frame, the frame has become resistant to correction.

That is frame management.

This is why the book distinguishes expansion and contraction.

The frame expands when it needs to explain more. It can absorb pain, fatigue, function, medication response, work limitation, and the general shape of the patient's life. It can become the large background against which everything is understood.

The frame contracts when a particular consequence would create difficulty. For example, an intervention may be described as relevant only to a narrow feature such as tremor. A symptom may be treated as not truly part of Parkinson's disease, even while Parkinson's is said to magnify it. A medication response may be accepted as real but not allowed to reopen the earlier explanatory separation.

Again, the point is not that any single contraction is necessarily wrong.

The point is that the frame controls the terms on which facts can matter.

A patient caught inside such a frame may experience a strange kind of recognition. He is not told that nothing is happening. He is not always told that his symptoms are imaginary. He may be told that his pain is real, that his suffering is understood, that medication may help, that the disease is complex, that treatment must be balanced carefully.

But if none of that recognition can alter the frame, recognition becomes containment.

The record says, in effect: we accept that this is happening, but we do not accept that it changes the governing structure.

That is why the phrase "documented without governing authority" belongs beside "frame management." They describe the same mechanism from different angles. From the patient's side, he is documented but not governing. From the institution's side, the frame remains governing even when it documents him.

This is not a claim that clinicians should abandon diagnostic frames whenever patients disagree. That would be unsafe and unrealistic. Medicine cannot be governed simply by patient preference. A patient may be mistaken about cause, mechanism, or treatment.

But neither can a diagnostic frame become immune from the patient's consistent account of the original problem.

The proper question is not whether the patient or the clinician automatically controls the meaning. The proper question is whether the record preserved a disciplined route by which the patient's account could test the frame.

If the patient repeatedly returns to the feet, and the feet were central from the beginning, the frame should have to answer the feet.

Not gesture toward them.

Not merely classify them.

Answer them, or preserve them as unanswered.

This is where the case becomes legally and administratively significant. Frame management can affect informed consent, because a patient cannot understand choices if the provisionality of the frame is not made clear. It can affect negligence analysis, because a diagnostic pathway may be reasonable only if uncertainty is handled properly. It can affect data accuracy, because historical entries may become misleading if later relied on without qualification. It can affect complaint handling, because a challenge to the frame may be misread as a mere disagreement with clinical judgment. It can affect professional accountability, because scope limits, referral duties, and correction duties may be hidden by the apparent completeness of the record.

A strong frame can therefore produce institutional inertia.

Once the frame has organised the record, later review may begin from the frame rather than from the original question. That is a reversal of sequence. The reviewer may ask whether later facts are compatible with Parkinson's disease instead of asking whether Parkinson's disease ever fully accounted for the presenting problem.

That reversal matters.

If the starting point is the frame, the feet become a problem to be fitted.

If the starting point is the referral, the frame becomes an answer to be tested.

This book insists on the second order.

The open problem came first. The frame came after. Therefore the frame must remain answerable to the problem.

Frame management should also be distinguished from fraud or bad faith. Nothing in this concept requires alleging that anyone deliberately manipulated the record. A frame can manage meaning without anyone intending to deceive. Institutional habits can do the work. Specialist language can do the work. Diagnostic confidence can do the work. Complaint categories can do the work. Data-protection formalism can do the work.

That is why the safer and stronger argument is structural.

The question is not, "who meant to trap the patient inside the frame?"

The question is, "what features of the record allowed the frame to become resistant to correction?"

That question is document-led. It does not depend on speculation about motive. It depends on sequence, contrast, repetition, and use.

A reader should therefore watch for the frame's behaviour.

Does it make room for facts, or does it domesticate them?

Does it preserve uncertainty, or merely display it?

Does it allow later contradiction to reopen the inquiry, or convert contradiction into another feature of the same explanation?

Does it treat the patient's account as evidence capable of governing the case, or as material to be interpreted by the case?

These are not academic questions. They are practical questions about the life of a record.

The patient lived not only with symptoms, but with the meanings assigned to those symptoms. Those meanings affected treatment, self-understanding, complaint pathways, and the possibility of correction. If the governing frame could absorb each return of the feet without changing, then the patient was not merely living with disease.

He was living under an interpretation.

This chapter gives that interpretation a name.

Frame management is the method by which a provisional diagnostic frame becomes durable, not by deleting contrary facts, but by controlling what those facts are permitted to mean.

That is why the case is not only about diagnosis.

It is about interpretive authority.

12. Complaint, Data, and Governance Routes

This book does not decide the legal route.

It prepares the reader to see why more than one route may matter.

A diagnostic-governance case does not fit neatly into one box. It may look, at first, like a clinical negligence issue. It may also involve consent, disclosure, referral, scope of expertise, data accuracy, complaints handling, administrative fairness, professional standards, and institutional learning. Each route asks a different question. Each has its own threshold, limitation rules, evidential demands, remedies, and risks.

The danger is to collapse them.

If everything is treated as negligence, the record problem may be missed. If everything is treated as data protection, the clinical sequence may be flattened. If everything is treated as complaint mishandling, the diagnostic reliance may disappear. If everything is treated as professional misconduct, the institutional structure may be reduced to personal blame.

The stronger approach is to keep the routes distinct while showing that they arise from the same documentary sequence.

The clinical route asks whether the care, investigation, referral, diagnosis, treatment, explanation, or follow-up met the applicable standard. That is not for this book to decide. It requires expert evidence. It requires attention to what was known at the time, what reasonable clinicians would have done, and whether any breach caused actionable harm.

The clinical route must be handled carefully. The book should not say that every act of care was negligent. It should not say that Parkinsonian features were invented. It should not say that later genetic confirmation automatically proves earlier breach. It should ask a more disciplined question: was it reasonable to allow durable reliance to attach while the original foot-centred problem, atypicality, genetic suspicion, scope limitation, and treatment-response questions remained unresolved or insufficiently qualified?

That is a question counsel and experts must test.

The disclosure route asks what the patient was told, what he was not told, and what he needed to know in order to understand the provisional nature of the frame. It may concern diagnosis, uncertainty, alternative explanations, treatment deferral, referral options, genetic possibilities, and the meaning of later medication choices.

Disclosure matters because a patient cannot participate in uncertainty he is not allowed to see. If the record contains uncertainty but the patient's practical understanding is that the matter has been settled, then documentation has not produced informed participation. The patient may be copied into letters and still not understand that a crucial route remains untested or that a frame has not been earned in the way its administrative force suggests.

The data route asks a different question.

A historical record may accurately show what was written or believed at the time. But continued reliance on that record can still become misleading if later developments make qualification necessary. Data accuracy is not only about whether a sentence was once written by a clinician. It may also concern whether the record, as processed and relied upon now, is incomplete, materially misleading, or insufficiently contextualised.

This is why erasure is not the natural centre of the case. The old record matters. It shows the sequence. It shows uncertainty and finality together. The more coherent data issue is whether the record now requires correction, qualification, supplementation, restriction, or contextualisation so that future reliance does not treat provisional or incomplete material as settled.

A correctable record does not need to pretend the past did not happen.

It needs to prevent the past from governing falsely.

The complaint route asks how the institution responded when the patient challenged the record. A complaint may be wrongly narrowed if it is treated only as dissatisfaction with clinical judgment. The patient's concern may be broader: that the record became durable without preserving a meaningful route back to correction.

If a complaint handler asks only whether a clinician acted within judgment, the complaint may miss its own subject. The issue may not be simply "was the doctor entitled to think this?" The issue may be "did the institution allow that thinking to acquire administrative and interpretive finality while the record itself preserved reasons for caution?"

That is a governance question.

The Ombudsman or maladministration route may therefore concern process, fairness, classification, escalation, delay, record handling, and whether the complaint was placed into the right procedural frame. It may ask whether the institution treated a structural challenge as a clinical disagreement. It may ask whether later reopening signals were acknowledged only to be contained.

The professional standards route is narrower and should not be inflated. It may concern scope, candour, referral, communication, record accuracy, and responsibility for correction. It should not be used casually as a substitute for proof. The book should not imply misconduct merely because the patient suffered or because later events disturbed the earlier frame. Professional accountability requires discipline. But where a clinician acknowledges complexity or scope limitation, counsel may properly ask what responsibilities followed.

The public-interest route is wider.

A case like this may teach something beyond one patient. It may show how a system can document uncertainty while still letting finality take hold. It may show how patients become dependent on records they did not control. It may show how complaint systems can defend documentation without asking whether documentation remained correctable. It may show how later contradiction can be absorbed rather than used to improve the record.

That does not turn the book into a campaign. It keeps the institutional lesson visible.

The routes can therefore be summarised this way.

Clinical negligence asks: was the care legally and medically adequate?

Disclosure asks: was the patient properly informed of uncertainty, options, and deferral?

Data protection asks: is continued reliance on the record accurate, complete, and fairly contextualised?

Complaints governance asks: was the challenge classified, investigated, and escalated properly?

Professional standards ask: did the conduct meet duties of competence, candour, referral, and correction?

Public-interest learning asks: what does the sequence reveal about institutional corrigibility?

These routes overlap, but they are not identical.

The book should keep them separate because separation improves force. A court may reject one route and still find another important. A regulator may not decide negligence but may still see a record-governance problem. A data controller may not adjudicate clinical truth but may still have to address misleading continued reliance. A complaints body may not substitute its clinical judgment but may still examine whether the complaint was mishandled.

The common thread is reliance.

In every route, the question returns: what was the record allowed to do?

Did it merely preserve a historical opinion?

Or did it continue to govern the patient, the institution, and later readers as though the uncertainty had been resolved?

The common remedy is not revenge.

It is correction.

Correction may mean an addendum. It may mean a qualification. It may mean an independent review. It may mean restriction of reliance. It may mean disclosure of records. It may mean expert examination of the sequence. It may mean an institutional statement that certain historical conclusions should no longer be read without later context. It may mean a finding that the complaint was wrongly framed.

The legal route will decide the available remedy.

But the principle is stable.

A patient should not be trapped under a record that preserved reasons for doubt while allowing those reasons to lose force.

That is why complaint, data, and governance routes belong in the same compact book. They are not distractions from the clinical case. They show how a clinical frame becomes institutional reality.

The diagnosis is the beginning.

Reliance is the problem.

Corrigibility is the test.

13. What Counsel Must Test

This book does not tell counsel what to conclude.

It tells counsel what must be tested.

The distinction is essential. A compact handbook should not pretend to be an expert report, a pleading, or an advice on proofs. Its work is to make the structure legible enough that counsel can decide what evidence is needed, what routes are viable, what claims are too weak, and what language should be avoided.

The first question is sequence.

What exactly was the order of events between referral, admission, diagnosis, discharge, LTI certification, DAT scan addendum, treatment decisions, later medication changes, complaint correspondence, data requests, and genetic confirmation? Did administrative or statutory finality attach before the inquiry had been completed or adequately qualified? Did later documents rely on earlier finality rather than reopening the original uncertainty?

The second question is the 2017 standard of care.

Given the patient's age, presentation, abnormal gait, limited tremor, symmetry, dystonic-looking features, foot-centred symptoms, possible genetic disease, and work-threatening deterioration, what would a reasonable clinician or service have done at that time? Would movement-disorders referral have been expected? Would genetics referral or testing have been expected? Would express qualification of the diagnosis have been expected? Would further foot-focused examination or documentation have been expected?

Those questions require expert evidence. They should not be answered by rhetoric.

The third question is the significance of the feet.

Were the feet adequately examined, explained, referred, photographed, described, or preserved as an unresolved primary feature? Were they treated as podiatric, neuropathic, dystonic, Parkinsonian, mechanical, mixed, or separate? Was the move from the patient's foot-centred presentation to the institution's Parkinsonian frame documented clearly enough to justify reliance?

The point is not that the patient must be right about the feet.

The point is that the record must be accountable to the feet.

The fourth question is treatment deferral.

Was it reasonable to defer levodopa or dopamine agonist therapy in a young patient because of dyskinesia, impulse-control, or long-term treatment concerns? If so, what should have followed from that deferral? Should the diagnosis have remained expressly provisional? Should the patient have been told that treatment response was being deferred and that this limited what could be known? Should later medication response have triggered a formal review of the earlier explanatory frame?

A treatment decision may be reasonable in isolation but problematic in combination with durable reliance.

The fifth question is scope.

What follows when a clinician records, or otherwise acknowledges, that the case lies near the edge of his or her movement-disorders expertise? Does that create a duty to refer? Does it require a second opinion? Does it require more explicit provisionality? Does it affect the safety of statutory or administrative finality?

A disclaimer is not necessarily a breach.

But counsel should test whether a disclaimer without a route to correction is enough.

The sixth question is genetics.

What was the significance, in 2017, of suspecting a possible autosomal-recessive or PRKN/PARK2-type presentation? What was available, appropriate, or reasonably expected then? Was delay reasonable? If delay was reasonable, did the record preserve the genetic question with enough force? When later genetic confirmation arrived, what correction or qualification of the historical record became necessary?

The later result must not be used as crude hindsight.

But it must not be treated as irrelevant either.

The seventh question is the DAT scan.

What did the DAT scan prove, and what did it not prove? Did it support a dopaminergic deficit? Did it distinguish idiopathic Parkinson's disease from genetic Parkinsonism, dystonia-related presentations, or other explanations? Did the timing of the addendum matter, particularly if administrative finality or discharge had already occurred? Did later reliance treat the scan as more conclusive than it was?

The eighth question is medication response.

What should be made of procyclidine response or withdrawal? What should be made of later Sinemet response? Did the medication response concern movement, pain, dystonia, walking initiation, or the experience of the feet? Was it ever used as a structured diagnostic review, or only as management? Did it disturb the earlier separation between Parkinsonian disease and foot pain?

The ninth question is DBS.

Was DBS accurately explained as relevant only to tremor? Was that statement correct in the patient's clinical context? Did it affect the patient's understanding of possible treatment routes? Did it contribute to the narrowing of options or to the durability of the existing frame?

This question should be tested cautiously. The book should not state that DBS was indicated. It should state that the recorded narrowing is a matter for expert review.

The tenth question is complaint handling.

When the patient complained, what was the complaint understood to be? Was it treated as disagreement with clinical judgment, or as a structural challenge to record reliance and corrigibility? Was the complaint escalated appropriately? Were the right people involved? Was independent review offered or avoided? Were later medication response and genetic developments treated as reopening signals or merely as details within the existing frame?

The eleventh question is data accuracy and continued reliance.

Are the historical records accurate only as historical entries, or are they now incomplete or misleading if processed without qualification? What correction, qualification, supplementation, restriction, or contextualisation is required? Was any restriction request properly handled? Were other recipients of the data or record notified if correction was necessary?

The twelfth question is causation and loss.

Even if an error, breach, or governance failure is established, what followed from it? What harm was caused by disease itself, and what harm was caused by premature finality, treatment delay, loss of opportunity, loss of autonomy, failure to refer, failure to correct, or prolonged reliance on an incomplete frame? Which losses are legally recoverable? Which are evidentially too uncertain?

This is where restraint matters most.

The book may show why the questions arise. It should not pretend they are all already answered.

The thirteenth question is limitation.

When did the patient know, or when could he reasonably have known, that the issue was not merely illness but a possible defect in the diagnostic sequence, disclosure, record handling, or complaint response? Did knowledge arise in 2017, later during treatment, in 2023 with crisis and misalignment, in March 2025 with medication response, or later still with genetic confirmation or data correspondence? Different routes may have different dates and different tests.

This is a legal question for counsel.

The fourteenth question is remedy.

What does the patient actually seek? Damages? Declaration? Correction? Addendum? Restriction of processing? Independent review? Complaint reopening? Referral? Institutional learning? Apology? Disclosure? A book like this should keep remedy clear because remedy disciplines the claim.

Not every wrong leads to the same remedy.

Not every remedy requires proving the same wrong.

The final question is language.

Counsel must decide how to frame the case without weakening it. The strongest language is not the loudest language. It is the language most faithful to the documents.

The patient was not simply ignored.

The diagnosis was not necessarily baseless.

Later genetics should not be projected backwards.

The record should not be erased.

The stronger formulation is this:

The institution recorded reasons why the diagnostic frame should have remained corrigible, but allowed that frame to acquire durable reliance before the originating problem had been resolved, falsified, or preserved as unresolved.

That is the proposition counsel must test.

If it survives expert and legal scrutiny, it gives the case its shape.

If it does not, the book should still have done its work: it will have identified the right questions, separated them from overclaim, and allowed the documents to be read in the order in which their force arose.

Legal safety box

This chapter identifies questions for counsel. It does not answer them.

The questions should not be converted into allegations without legal advice and, where necessary, expert evidence. The central proposition remains a review question:

Did the institution record reasons why the diagnostic frame should have remained corrigible while allowing that frame to acquire durable reliance before the originating problem had been resolved, falsified, or preserved as unresolved?

That is the question to be tested, not a pleaded conclusion.

Outline instruction to expert witness

An expert witness should not be asked to decide the legal case.

The expert should be asked to assist with the clinical and evidential questions that bear on the case theory. A suitable instruction may include the following:

Proposed core question: Please review the clinical records and advise whether, in your opinion, the diagnostic and treatment sequence remained clinically reasonable and adequately qualified, having regard to the patient's young age, atypical features, foot-centred presentation, dystonic-looking gait, possible genetic Parkinsonism, deferred levodopa or dopamine agonist therapy, later medication responses, recurrent severe foot pain, and eventual PRKN/PARK2 confirmation.

Please address in particular:

  1. What was the reasonable clinical standard in 2017 for assessment of this presentation?
  2. Was Parkinson's disease, young-onset Parkinsonism, or idiopathic Parkinson's disease a reasonable working diagnosis?
  3. If so, should that diagnosis have been qualified as provisional, atypical, genetic, incomplete, or subject to movement-disorders review?
  4. Were the patient's foot symptoms adequately examined, explained, documented, and followed?
  5. What was the significance of describing the gait as dystonic-looking?
  6. What was the significance of suspecting PRKN/PARK2 or autosomal-recessive disease in 2017?
  7. Was deferral of levodopa or dopamine agonist therapy reasonable, and what should have followed from that deferral?
  8. What did the DAT scan establish, and what did it not establish?
  9. What clinical significance attaches to procyclidine withdrawal and recurrence of the outside-left-foot phenomenon?
  10. What clinical significance attaches to later Sinemet responsiveness of the feet or foot pain?
  11. Did later records disclose a need to reopen or qualify the earlier diagnostic frame?
  12. What is the clinical significance of later PRKN/PARK2 confirmation?
  13. Did the patient lose a material opportunity for earlier diagnosis, referral, treatment, symptom relief, or more accurate understanding?
  14. If avoidable harm occurred, what harm is medically attributable to the sequence rather than to the underlying disease alone?

Important restraint: The expert should be asked to identify what can be concluded, what cannot be concluded, and what remains uncertain.

Expert safety box

The expert should not be asked to validate the book's theory.

The expert should be asked to test it.

A proper expert instruction should invite disagreement, alternative explanations, and identification of uncertainty. The expert should be free to conclude that some decisions were reasonable, that some later signals are not clinically significant, or that the record does not support a proposed inference.

The value of the expert evidence lies in independence.

14. Cautions Against Hindsight

The case is strongest when it is restrained.

That restraint is not politeness. It is method.

A record-governance case can be weakened by saying too much. It can be weakened by turning every uncertainty into proof, every omission into bad faith, every later development into retrospective certainty, and every clinical decision into negligence. That would not make the case stronger. It would make it easier to dismiss.

The documents carry the force.

The language must not outrun them.

The first caution is against hindsight.

Later knowledge matters, but it must be used correctly. Later PRKN/PARK2 genetic confirmation cannot simply be projected backwards into 2017 as though clinicians then possessed what was only confirmed later. That would convert a disciplined sequence case into a crude retrospective accusation.

The better point is narrower.

The earlier record already contained atypicality, young age, possible genetic disease, unresolved features, and treatment caution. Later confirmation matters because it returns to a path already visible in the record. It illuminates the importance of uncertainty that was already there. It does not create that uncertainty after the fact.

That distinction should be preserved everywhere.

The second caution is against saying the diagnosis was baseless.

The record does not support that. Parkinsonian features were recorded. Dopaminergic pathway abnormality was later identified. The clinical frame was not invented out of nothing. The case should not deny what the documents plainly contain.

The stronger proposition is that plausibility did not justify durable finality unless the unresolved features were adequately explained, falsified, escalated, or preserved as unresolved.

Plausible is not the same as complete.

The third caution is against alleging malice.

Nothing in this book requires malice. The case does not depend on proving that anyone set out to harm, deceive, silence, or trap the patient. Institutional failure can occur through ordinary mechanisms: diagnostic confidence, specialist hierarchy, record templates, administrative reliance, complaint classification, data formalism, and failure to reopen.

Motive is not the centre.

Sequence is the centre.

The fourth caution is against saying the patient was simply ignored.

That is not accurate enough. The patient was recorded, examined, counselled, copied, described, and answered. His intelligence was recognised. His symptoms were written down. His suffering was acknowledged. His medication response was eventually accepted.

The better formulation is that he was documented without governing authority.

That phrase should remain one of the book's central safeguards. It avoids overclaim and captures the deeper problem. The patient was not absent. He was subordinated within the hierarchy of explanation.

The fifth caution is against turning every clinical act into negligence.

A reader may feel anger when the sequence becomes clear. That is understandable. But anger is not proof. Some decisions may have been reasonable. Some may have been reasonable only if properly qualified. Some may require expert review. Some may be irrelevant to legal liability even if they matter humanly.

The book should preserve the difference between a question and a conclusion.

Counsel must test breach, causation, limitation, loss, and remedy. The book prepares the question. It does not pretend to answer all of it.

The sixth caution is against erasure.

The remedy should not be framed as wiping the old record clean. Historical records matter. They show what was thought, what was done, what was deferred, what was omitted, what was relied upon, and what remained unresolved. Erasure would risk destroying the very sequence that proves the problem.

The better language is correction, qualification, supplementation, restriction, and contextualisation.

A correctable record does not destroy history.

It prevents history from governing falsely.

The seventh caution is against collapsing routes.

Clinical negligence, consent, data protection, maladministration, professional standards, and public-interest learning are not the same route. They may overlap. They may depend on the same documents. But each asks a different question and leads to different remedies.

If the routes are collapsed, the case becomes vague.

If they are separated, the structure becomes stronger.

The eighth caution is against overloading the book with evidence.

The full record is large. The book is small. Its job is not to reproduce everything. Its job is to teach the reader how to read the record. A compact handbook must resist the temptation to become a document dump. Too much evidence can obscure the sequence.

The book should select the moments that carry the structure: open referral, discharge hinge, LTI/statutory reliance, DAT timing, treatment deferral, scope limitation, foot recurrence, Sinemet response, 2023 crisis, misalignment, complaint handling, data correspondence, and genetic confirmation.

The ninth caution is against replacing the patient's history with the case.

The patient has already named the point. This is his medical experience and part of his history. It is not his story. The book should not consume the person. It should not present suffering as identity. It should not reduce a life to a file.

The humane force of the book lies partly in that refusal.

The tenth caution is against certainty about what expert evidence must say.

The book may identify questions. It may show tensions. It may point to unresolved features. It may show why counsel review is needed. But it should not speak as though the expert conclusions are already known. If expert evidence is required, the text should say so.

That restraint protects the book.

The eleventh caution is against treating documentation as defeat.

The institution may point to extensive documentation. That matters. But it does not answer the core issue. The book should accept the documentation, then ask what the documentation did. Did it preserve uncertainty as operative? Did it qualify reliance? Did it support correction? Or did it become the mechanism by which the frame hardened?

The twelfth caution is against treating apology as resolution.

An apology may matter humanly. It may matter institutionally. But an apology does not necessarily correct a record, reopen a sequence, qualify reliance, or answer governance questions. The book should not dismiss apology. It should not overvalue it either.

The thirteenth caution is against moral simplification.

This is not a simple story of good patient and bad doctor. It is more serious than that. It is about how a system can become overconfident through documents that themselves preserve reasons for caution. It is about how a provisional frame can become useful, then durable, then resistant to correction. It is about how a patient can trust the system and still later discover that the record he trusted has become the obstacle to correction.

That is the point.

The language should remain exact.

Do not say: they ignored him.

Say: he was documented without governing authority.

Do not say: the diagnosis was fabricated.

Say: the frame may have been plausible, but plausibility did not justify unqualified durable reliance.

Do not say: later genetics proves everything.

Say: later genetic confirmation returned to a line of uncertainty already visible in the record.

Do not say: erase the record.

Say: correct, qualify, supplement, restrict, and contextualise continued reliance.

Do not say: every clinical act was negligent.

Say: counsel and experts must test whether the sequence met the required standard.

Do not say: the case is obvious.

Say: the documents make the question unavoidable.

The aim is not to make the strongest noise.

It is to make the strongest record-faithful argument.

That is why hindsight must be resisted. Hindsight offers a false shortcut. It says: now that we know the later fact, the earlier failure is self-evident. But the better case does not need that shortcut. It says: before the later fact, the record already preserved reasons why the frame should have remained correctable.

That is the more disciplined claim.

It is also the more difficult one to dismiss.

15. Closing Note: The Right to a Correctable Record

A patient does not have a right to a perfect diagnosis.

Medicine could not function under that standard. Diagnosis is uncertain. Bodies are difficult. Symptoms overlap. Tests are incomplete. Clinicians must often act before everything is known. A record that honestly preserves uncertainty is not, for that reason alone, a bad record.

The right at stake is different.

It is the right not to be governed indefinitely by a record that preserved reasons why it should have remained corrigible.

That is the principle this book has tried to make visible.

The case began with an open problem. The patient came through feet, gait, stiffness, movement, function, and work-threatening deterioration. The record then formed a Parkinsonian frame. That frame may have been plausible. But the record also preserved atypicality, unresolved features, possible genetic disease, dystonic-looking gait, treatment caution, and later contradiction.

The question is not whether uncertainty existed.

The question is whether uncertainty was allowed to matter.

A correctable record does not require clinicians to be omniscient. It requires them, and the institutions around them, to preserve a route back from provisional finality when the record itself shows that finality has not been earned. It requires later facts to be capable of qualifying earlier reliance. It requires patient testimony to be more than material for interpretation. It requires complaint and data systems to hear a challenge to record-governance as more than disagreement with clinical judgment.

This is a modest principle.

It is also a demanding one.

It asks institutions to distinguish between writing something down and letting it govern. It asks them to distinguish between historical accuracy and present reliance. It asks them to distinguish between a diagnosis as a clinical hypothesis and a diagnosis as an administrative instrument. It asks them to notice when a frame has become too useful to correct.

That is not only a legal concern.

It is a human one.

A patient lives inside the meanings the record creates. Those meanings affect care, treatment, benefits, work, identity, family, complaint rights, and the ability to explain what happened. If the record becomes durable too early, the patient may spend years trying to return to the original question while the system keeps answering from the settled frame.

That is not simply a failure of communication.

It is a failure of corrigibility.

This book has used one case to describe that structure. It has not tried to resolve every legal issue. It has not tried to replace expert evidence. It has not tried to turn suffering into proof. It has tried to show why the documents matter, how they should be read, and why the central question deserves proper testing.

The final question is simple.

When the record itself preserves reasons for doubt, who bears responsibility for keeping the route to correction open?

The patient cannot do it alone. He does not control the clinical file. He does not control diagnostic language. He does not control coding, statutory forms, discharge summaries, complaint pathways, or data processing. He can speak. He can protest. He can ask. He can later complain. But if the system does not preserve his account as capable of governing, his words may enter the record only to be contained by it.

A correctable record protects against that.

It allows the past to remain visible without making the past untouchable. It allows clinicians to record what they believed while also allowing later developments to qualify what may now be safely relied upon. It allows uncertainty to travel with the diagnosis instead of being left behind in a paragraph no one acts upon. It allows the patient to return to the record without having to defeat the record before he can be heard.

That is the right this book names.

Not a right to certainty.

Not a right to hindsight.

Not a right to have every clinical judgment reversed.

A right to a record that can still be corrected when its own contents, and later events, show that reliance has become unsafe.

That is why the case should not be reduced to misdiagnosis.

It is about corrigibility and reliance.

It is about a provisional frame that became durable.

It is about documents that preserved reasons for caution while allowing finality to proceed.

It is about the difference between being recorded and being governed.

It is about the patient who trusted the system, did not keep score, and later had to rely on the documents to understand how the documents had come to govern him.

The closing proposition is therefore the same as the opening one.

The institution did not merely make a diagnosis; it allowed a provisional diagnostic frame to acquire durable reliance while the record itself preserved reasons why that reliance should have remained corrigible.

That sentence is not a verdict.

It is the question the record now requires.

16. Key Terms

This section defines the working vocabulary of the book.

The definitions are not offered as statutory definitions, clinical definitions, or final legal conclusions. They are reading tools. Their purpose is to help the reader follow how the record moved from uncertainty to reliance, and why correction became the central issue.

Corrigibility

Corrigibility is the capacity of a record, diagnosis, pathway, or institutional position to be corrected, qualified, reopened, supplemented, restricted, or contextualised when later information makes continued reliance unsafe.

It is not the same as admitting error. It is not the same as deleting the past. It is not the same as saying every earlier decision was wrong.

A corrigible record can say: this was the view at the time, but continued reliance on that view now requires qualification.

Corrigibility is the safeguard against premature finality.

Reliance

Reliance is the practical dependence placed on a record or diagnosis by clinicians, administrators, statutory schemes, complaint handlers, data controllers, later reviewers, and the patient himself.

A diagnosis becomes reliance-heavy when it does more than describe a clinical view. It begins to guide treatment, correspondence, certification, entitlement, interpretation, complaint handling, and institutional defence.

The question is not only what the diagnosis said.

The question is what the diagnosis was allowed to do.

Provisional Frame

A provisional frame is a working explanation that may be clinically plausible but has not yet earned durable authority.

A provisional frame may be necessary. Medicine often begins with provisional explanations. The problem arises when a provisional frame becomes practically final while the record still preserves reasons why it should remain open.

A provisional frame is not wrongful merely because it is provisional.

It becomes dangerous when provisionality is lost in use.

Operative Authority

Operative authority is the power of a fact, uncertainty, symptom, or patient account to affect what happens next.

A fact has operative authority when it changes the plan, limits reliance, triggers referral, preserves provisionality, prompts correction, or prevents premature closure.

A fact lacks operative authority when it is merely recorded.

The central problem in this case is not that uncertainty disappeared. It is that uncertainty lost operative authority.

Documentary Finality

Documentary finality occurs when a document, especially a discharge summary or formal letter, begins to function as settled even though its contents preserve unresolved questions.

A document may contain caveats and still perform finality through headings, diagnosis fields, medication plans, follow-up arrangements, certification, and repetition.

Documentary finality is often created by structure as much as by language.

Administrative Finality

Administrative finality is closure created through forms, coding, pathways, appointments, schemes, complaint handling, or institutional process.

It may follow from a clinical diagnosis but is not identical to it. Once a diagnosis enters administrative systems, it can become harder to correct because later actors rely on it as a settled status.

Administrative finality matters because it gives a clinical frame institutional durability.

Statutory Finality

Statutory finality occurs when a clinical frame is used for a legal, statutory, entitlement, certification, or formal administrative purpose.

This kind of finality can make a provisional diagnosis more durable. It can also make later correction more difficult because the diagnosis has been translated into formal status.

The issue is not that statutory forms should never be completed.

The issue is whether such completion should occur while the underlying inquiry remains materially unresolved or insufficiently qualified.

Reopening Signal

A reopening signal is a later fact that should prompt reconsideration, qualification, referral, correction, or renewed analysis of an earlier frame.

A reopening signal is not proof. It is not a verdict. It does not decide breach or causation by itself.

It asks whether the system remained capable of correction.

Examples may include recurrence of a foot phenomenon, medication response, severe return of pain, recorded misalignment, genetic confirmation, complaint escalation, or contradiction between earlier explanation and later experience.

Frame Management

Frame management is the process by which later facts are interpreted through an existing diagnostic frame.

Frame management may be legitimate. All clinical reasoning requires interpretation.

It becomes problematic when the frame controls what later facts are permitted to mean, so that contradiction is absorbed rather than allowed to reopen the inquiry.

A frame manages meaning when it expands to explain facts that support it and contracts when facts would disturb it.

Documented Without Governing Authority

"Documented without governing authority" describes the position of a patient whose symptoms, concerns, intelligence, correspondence, and suffering are recorded, but whose account does not control the hierarchy of explanation.

It is more precise than saying the patient was ignored.

The patient may be present throughout the record and still not be allowed to govern the record.

This phrase is central because it captures both recognition and subordination.

Operationally Misleading Record

An operationally misleading record is a record that may accurately preserve what was written or believed at a time, but becomes misleading when later relied upon without qualification.

The problem is not always historical falsity.

The problem may be present reliance.

A record can be accurate as archive and misleading as governance.

Hindsight Claim

A hindsight claim wrongly projects later knowledge backwards as though it had been available earlier.

This book avoids that method.

Later PRKN/PARK2 confirmation matters not because it proves what clinicians knew in 2017, but because the earlier record already contained genetic suspicion, atypicality, and unresolved features. Later confirmation returns to a line of inquiry already visible in the file.

The proper use of later evidence is to test corrigibility, not to manufacture retrospective certainty.

Source-Record Discipline

Source-record discipline means keeping the evidence and the analysis visibly separate.

The documents should be allowed to speak first. Interpretation should follow the record, not replace it.

This discipline protects the book from overclaim. It also helps counsel, experts, and readers see where the argument comes from.

Finality

Finality is the point at which a diagnosis, explanation, or institutional position begins to function as settled.

Finality can be clinical, documentary, administrative, statutory, interpretive, complaint-based, or data-based.

Finality is not always wrong.

The question is whether finality was earned.

Correction

Correction means more than changing a word.

It may include amendment, addendum, qualification, restriction, supplementation, contextualisation, notification, or independent review.

Correction is the practical expression of corrigibility.

Qualification

Qualification is a form of correction that preserves the historical record while limiting what may safely be inferred from it.

A qualified record may say: this diagnosis or explanation was recorded at the time, but later facts or unresolved features mean it should not be read as complete or final without context.

Qualification is especially important where erasure would be inaccurate but unqualified reliance would be misleading.

Restriction

Restriction means limiting the use or onward reliance of a record, diagnosis, or data entry while accuracy, completeness, or legal processing is disputed.

In this book, restriction belongs with correction and qualification. It is one of the ways a patient may seek to prevent disputed material from continuing to govern without context.

Contextualisation

Contextualisation means adding enough surrounding explanation to prevent a historical record from being misread.

A record may need contextualisation where later events change how earlier entries should be understood, even if the original entries remain historically important.

Contextualisation protects both history and fairness.

Solicitor / Counsel Orientation Note

This note may accompany The Correctable Record if the handbook is sent for legal review.

Purpose of the handbook

The attached handbook is a compact orientation document concerning a medical-record and diagnostic-governance sequence.

It is not a pleading, legal advice, medical advice, expert evidence, or a complete chronology. It is intended to help counsel identify the issues that may require legal analysis, medical expert opinion, data-protection review, complaint-governance review, or further documentary proof.

The central proposition for review is:

The institution did not merely make a diagnosis; it allowed a provisional diagnostic frame to acquire durable reliance while the record itself preserved reasons why that reliance should have remained corrigible.

This proposition is offered as an issue for testing, not as a pleaded conclusion.

Requested review

Counsel is asked to consider whether the records support any viable route concerning:

  1. clinical negligence;
  2. informed consent or disclosure;
  3. failure to refer, escalate, or obtain appropriate specialist input;
  4. negligent or premature diagnostic finality;
  5. loss of opportunity for earlier diagnosis, treatment, symptom relief, or accurate understanding;
  6. data accuracy, correction, restriction, or contextualisation;
  7. complaint handling or maladministration;
  8. professional standards issues;
  9. limitation;
  10. remedy.

Central factual structure

The proposed structure is as follows.

The patient entered the hospital pathway through an open GP referral concerning abnormal gait, bilateral foot problems, stiffness, movement difficulty, dexterity decline, and work-threatening deterioration.

A Parkinsonian frame then formed. The handbook does not deny that Parkinsonian features were recorded or that such a frame may have been clinically plausible.

The concern is that the frame acquired durable clinical, administrative, statutory, and interpretive reliance while the record also preserved features that may have required ongoing qualification: young age, atypicality, limited tremor, dystonic-looking gait, possible genetic disease, unresolved foot and leg symptoms, treatment deferral, and later contradiction.

The requested review is therefore not simply: was there a misdiagnosis?

The requested review is: did the record remain meaningfully correctable once reliance attached?

Issues likely requiring expert evidence

The following issues appear to require medical expert review:

  1. The appropriate 2017 standard of care for a young patient with atypical Parkinsonian features and a foot-centred gait presentation.
  2. Whether the initial hospital assessment adequately addressed the feet, gait, dystonic-looking features, and differential diagnosis.
  3. Whether Parkinson's disease, young-onset Parkinsonism, idiopathic Parkinson's disease, genetic Parkinsonism, dystonia, neuropathy, musculoskeletal pathology, or mixed pathology were properly considered and distinguished.
  4. Whether the diagnosis should have been expressly qualified as provisional, atypical, genetic, incomplete, or subject to movement-disorders review.
  5. Whether levodopa or dopamine agonist deferral was reasonable and, if so, what qualification or review should have followed.
  6. What the DAT scan established and what it did not establish.
  7. The significance of procyclidine response or withdrawal.
  8. The significance of later Sinemet responsiveness affecting the feet or foot pain.
  9. The clinical significance of recurrent severe bilateral foot pain.
  10. The significance of later PRKN/PARK2 confirmation.
  11. Whether earlier referral, testing, treatment, or qualification would probably or materially have altered outcome, understanding, treatment, or suffering.
  12. Whether the record, as a clinical record, required later correction or qualification.

Issues for legal analysis

The following issues require legal analysis and should not be treated as answered by the handbook:

  1. Duty and breach.
  2. Causation.
  3. Recoverable loss.
  4. Limitation and date of knowledge.
  5. Consent and disclosure.
  6. Data-protection accuracy, rectification, restriction, and fair processing.
  7. Complaint-governance standards.
  8. Available remedies.
  9. Evidential burden.
  10. Appropriate defendants, respondents, or bodies.

Important cautions

The handbook deliberately avoids the following overclaims:

  • It does not say the original diagnosis was baseless.
  • It does not allege malice.
  • It does not say every clinical act was negligent.
  • It does not project later genetic confirmation backwards as though it were known in 2017.
  • It does not seek erasure of the historical record.
  • It does not treat patient suffering as proof of breach.
  • It does not ask an expert to validate a desired conclusion.

The safer formulation is:

The documents create questions that require disciplined review.
